* atmosphere
	* Overall Tone:
		* The world is stark and unforgiving, designed to keep the player slightly on edge. Each challenge serves as a reminder of the player character’s precarious place within this environment.
		* The game balances tension with fleeting moments of humor or wonder, creating emotional highs and lows that mirror the harsh but complex nature of the setting.
		* An adversarial spirit is embedded into the game itself, subtly positioning it as an opposing force. The game’s world doesn’t "want" the player to survive but also doesn’t actively intervene in obvious ways, lending it a brooding indifference that feels quietly hostile.
	* Adversarial Spirit:
		* Survival as a Battle of Wills: The player’s true adversary is the world itself. It manifests in relentless challenges: harsh weather, dwindling resources, and a landscape that is both ancient and ever-changing.
		* Sparse Exposition: Minimal hand-holding and few explanations make the player feel as though they’re constantly missing information, intensifying the sense of a hostile or indifferent universe.
		* Nature as a Character: The environment is a silent opponent—trees seem to loom, shadows twist in unnatural ways, and buildings groan as if resentful of intrusion.
	* Primary Atmosphere - Spooky:
		* Visual and Descriptive Tone: The world, especially Philadelphia in this distant future, is tinged with a feeling of haunted history. There’s an eerie sense of places abandoned for too long, with everyday items or remnants of the old world now feeling alien and unsettling.
		* Sounds and Silence: The game might use brief sound descriptors like "a faint rustling nearby" or "a distant, hollow clang," heightening the tension in sparse descriptions.
		* Familiarity Made Strange: Broken-down infrastructure and relics of present-day life are now enigmatic artifacts, their purposes lost to time, adding an eerie layer to exploration.
	* Secondary Atmosphere - Mysterious:
		* Layered Narratives: The world suggests stories without fully telling them. Unmarked graves, strange symbols, and old machinery invite curiosity, rewarding players who dig deeper into lore.
		* Discovery of the Unseen: Traps, technology, and old documents are encoded with mysteries that the player unravels, sometimes to their detriment, adding a feeling that truth here comes with risk.
		* Secrets Unfolding: Encounters and settings subtly reveal the past through surreal encounters, providing tantalizing but incomplete glimpses of the world’s history and hinting at what went wrong.
	* Tertiary Atmosphere - Foreboding:
		* Sense of Imminent Threat: There’s always a looming sense that something could go wrong. Even in “safe” areas, there’s a feeling that the peace is temporary or conditional.
		* Environmental Triggers: Players may encounter signs of impending danger—clouds darkening, strange weather patterns, animals acting strangely—all hinting that the world is gearing up for something dire.
		* Unfinished Business: As players progress, they encounter remnants of other adventurers or communities that almost survived. This undercurrent of failed attempts creates an uneasy feeling that they’re following in the footsteps of those who did not make it.
	* Moments of Levity:
		* Dark or Dry Humor: Oddball characters may have a dry sense of humor or offer sly observations about the absurdity of the world, helping to break tension.
		* Whimsical Artifacts: Some relics or pieces of “magic” (advanced tech) have unintended functions or bizarre effects, providing rare, lighthearted moments in the otherwise grim journey.
		* NPCs with Quirks: The few allies or informants the player encounters may have distinct, quirky personalities that contrast with the desolate world, offering refreshing, albeit brief, companionship.
	* Moments of Surrealism:
		* Nature as a Force Beyond Understanding: The flora and fauna of this far-future Philadelphia exhibit strange, otherworldly characteristics—plants may hum at night, stones emit faint glows, and animals behave with eerie intelligence.
		* Techno-Magic Anomalies: Some of the “magic” (technology) the player interacts with may behave unpredictably, creating strange experiences that defy logic. An artifact may temporarily distort perception or transport the player to a vision of the past.
		* Reality Slippage: Occasionally, as players dig deeper, they experience moments that feel disjointed, where past and present collide, or where environments subtly shift, hinting at a fractured or layered reality.

	* predefined set of commands
		* look: Provides a description of your current surroundings, including notable items, exits, and any characters present.
		* move [direction]: Allows the player to navigate the game world in a specified direction (e.g., north, south, east, west).
		* examine [object]: Gives a detailed description of a specific object in the environment, helping players understand its significance or how to interact with it.
		* take [object]: Allows the player to pick up an item and add it to their inventory.
		* drop [object]: Lets the player drop an item from their inventory back into the environment.
		* inventory: Displays a list of items currently held by the player.
		* use [object]: Activates or utilizes an item from the player's inventory, which could be essential for solving puzzles.
		* talk to [character]: Initiates a conversation with a non-player character (NPC), leading to potential quests or information.
		* search [area/object]: Looks for hidden items or clues in a specific area or object, which may yield unexpected results.
		* craft: Enters a menu for crafting items
		* combat commands: fight (enters fight menu), flee (leaves the fight with consequences), item (enters an inventory menu)
		* menu: enters main menu with options including
			* map
			* commands
		* where am i: describes location (region, relative distance and cardinal direction to safe zone/economic zone)
		* hide: attempts to hide or otherwise conceal presence
		* fight [character]: initiates combat
		* say [content]: says the contents of [content] (to the character being spoken to if in dialogue, else just into the air)
		* help: Provides a brief overview of available commands and their functions.
		* hint: gives a hint about best next move
		* save: Saves the current game state, allowing players to return to this point later.
		* load: Loads a previously saved game state.
		* quit: Exits the game, asking for confirmation before closing.
	* combat
		* encounter setup and frequency
			* probability/zone system:
				* safe zone: no chance of combat encounter
					* there is a primary "base" in each region with precisely zero chance of combat encounter
				* mobile safety:
					* "conveyance" referred to by npc's at varying points as both a machine and a beast, as both being driven and being ridden
					* highwaymen may fight, and depending on how nice the thing looks the chance of this will go up.  but they are much much safer in/on this than walking
					* this will resolve weight limits but then the player has to choose what to bring out with them on excursions
				* economic zone
					* there is an economic zone in each region with things like shops
					* in this zone there is an extremely low but non-zero chance of encounter
					* social tension
						* some encounters are non-combatant interactions that might escalate depending on the players choice or reputation in this zone
				* medium: the first unit of distance away from safety, medium encounter frequency
				* danger zone: highest chance of encounter
				* cave/dungeon: chance increases as the dungeon progresses
			* some special encounters are scripted or triggered by story progression to ensure specific plot moments
		* nature of combat/enemies
			* wildlife, environmental hazards
				* react to player actions
					* loud noises, food presence
			* desperate npc's
				* some may be open to negotiation
				* others act erratically
			* emphasis on avoiding conflict or creative problem solving
				* stealth, negotiation, using found objects
			* minimal, turn based combat
				* [Fight] [Flee] [Items]
				* Submenus under [Fight] and [Items] are intentionally simple
			* only the pc selects actions, npc alliles auto attack or assist based on set tactics
			* initiative
				* pre-determined each encounter
				* more powerful enemies get higher mods to their initiative rolls
		* factors affecting combat
			* weather
			* visibility
			* resources
				* each encounter impacts resources (health, tools)
					* reinforces the need to avoid unnecessary fights
		* combat methods
			* weapon attack
				* weapons weaken over time before eventually being destroyed
			* melee attack
				* takes energy - defined by str or con
			* magic attack
				* takes focus - defined by int or wis
		* rewards
			* experience, but this is not the only way to level up
			* resources, but this is not the only way to get resources
				* but it may be a good way to get resources which are "higher level" (read: require more resources to craft or purchase) than the players current state would allow
	* economic system
		* resources
			* base: water
				* low scarcity
				* no barter value
				* weight: 1 unit
			* common: scrap material
				* Description: Random, salvaged parts from machines (e.g., screws, metal shards, old wires, wood).
				* Uses: Repairs, minor crafting, simple weapons, basic traps, and barter with lower-tier NPCs.
				* Scarcity Level: Low.
				* Barter Value: 1 unit.
				* Weight: 1 unit
			* moderate: food rations
				* Description: Preserved and easily portable food items (canned goods, dried meats, grains).
				* Uses: Essential for survival and health regeneration; high trade value in all zones.
				* Scarcity Level: Moderate; limited to specific zones and encounters.
				* Barter Value: 5 units
				* weight: 2 units
			* uncommon: medical supplies
				* Description: Basic first aid, bandages, antiseptics, pain relievers. Essential for recovery and minor healing.
				* Uses: Restores health, crucial for sustaining longer exploration and surviving encounters.
				* Scarcity Level: Uncommon; frequently found in economic zones or near settlements but rare in hostile areas.
				* Barter Value: 10 units.
				* weight: 3 units
			* Rare: Fuel and Energy Cells
				* Description: Old fuel canisters, batteries, or specialized cells needed for running machinery and transportation.
				* Uses: Powers conveyance, lights, or small machinery; has high barter value with specialized NPCs.
				* Scarcity Level: Rare; found in high-danger zones or from specific NPCs in exchanges.
				* Barter Value: 25 units.
				* weight: 4 units
			* Very Rare: Ancient Relics
				* Description: Unique and mysterious items from before the collapse (e.g., an old watch, rare jewelry, strange technological artifacts).
				* Uses: Mostly valuable for bartering, unlocking certain story elements, or as powerful talismans.
				* Scarcity Level: Very rare; found only in hidden or high-risk zones.
				* Barter Value: 50 units.
				* weight: 5 units
		* inventory
			* initial strict weight limit
				* start at 12 units and increase by 2 per level
				* npc adds 5 units and increase by 1 per level
				* items need to be stored in safe zones – this means that until the conveyance is unlocked, every new region is like starting from near scratch as you can't take items with you beyond the weight limit per character
			* conveyance
				* unlock by mid-game (this is the event that defines "mid-game")
				* weight limit on excursion from conveyance is still there, but this removes the weight limit outside the safe zone
				* encounter chance goes up as the weight stored in the conveyance goes up
				* needs fuel – prior to the conveyance being unlocked, fuel is only a bartering item and it is not used otherwise
		* exchange rates
			* Core Exchange Table: Behind the scenes, each resource has a base value (as noted above) used for general bartering.
			* NPC Preferences: Certain NPCs may value specific resources higher based on their needs (e.g., a traveling healer values medical supplies more highly than fuel).
			* Fluctuations: Events in the game can shift demand (e.g., droughts make food scarcer, making it more valuable in certain zones).
		* specialized trades
			* item combinations
				* players can suggest bartering items in combination to get higher tier resources
			* high value bartering
				* high value npcs only accept ancient relics in exchange for unique items, hints, or rare/story progressing supplies
	* trap system
		* key resource gathering mechanic
		* resources can be gathered manually, but this improves efficiency
		* items gained from these traps immediately transport to the safe zone inventory
		* types
			* small trap
				* early game
				* cost 1 scrap
					* therefore weigh 1 unit
				* one time use 
				* yield scrap/water -> food -> medical
				* expire 1 day/night cycle after being set
			* big trap
				* mid game
				* cost 2 scrap, 1 food
					* therefore weigh 4 units
				* yield scrap/water/food -> medical -> energy -> relics
				* one tiem use
				* expire 2 day/night cycle after being set
			* defense trap
				* end game
				* set behind you on excursions
				* cost 3 scrap, 2 fuel
					* therefore weigh 11 units
				* yield scrap/water/food/medical -> energy -> relic
				* one time use
				* expire 3 day/night cycle after being set
		* efficiency is higher at night
		* higher risk higher reward
			* placing them somewhere dangerous will increase cahnce of success
		* effectiveness changes in different areas
		* when traps expire or succeed, a message comes up notifying you

			* gain for defeating enemies or resolving conflict
